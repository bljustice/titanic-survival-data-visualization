Titanic Passenger Survival Status based on Age Group, Gender, and Passenger Class

Summary

This visualization shows three different Titanic passenger features and how they affected that passenger's survival status after the ship crashed. The three features are age group, gender and passenger group. Each feature can be loaded individually by selecting one of the radio buttons below. There are 891 passengers shown in the gender and passenger class charts, while the age group chart shows 714 passengers. The reason that there are less passengers in the age group visualization is because several passengers did not have a reported age. I decided to exclude them from this to make the bar chart easier to read.

Conclusion

From a high level, there are three very apparent conclusions that can be drawn from the data, which have been listed below.

  1. The majority of passengers did not survive after the Titanic crashed.
  2. Females had a higher chance of survival. This was most likely caused by the fact that women and children were moved off the boat first after it crashed.
  3. Upper class passengers had a higher chance of survival than the lower class did. This was most likely caused by the upper class being unloaded before the other two classes were, and that the lower class (class 3) did not have lifeboats.

A couple of other specific findings from this visualization can be found below. These trends are based on the sample dataset used. All percentages are rounded to the nearest whole number.

  • Of all passengers in the age group dataset, 54% were between 20-40 years old. Of that group, 40% survived.
  • The majority of passengers were in passenger class 3, which was the lowest class. Of that group, 24% survived.
  • The majority of male passengers did not survive (81% died), but the majority of females did (26% died).
  • The passenger group with most survivors was group 1, which was considered the highest class group. This group made up 24% of all passengers, and 63% of them survived.
